The [sourcing page](/en/hardware/sourcing/) split the build into three buckets. This page is all
of **bucket C**: the custom parts no vendor stocks, the ones you produce yourself.

"Produce yourself" does **not** mean print at home. PhysiClaw's custom parts are load-bearing fit
parts — they carry the moving gantry, position the idlers, and hold the solenoid square to the
phone — so they have to come out dimensionally true. That rules out a desktop FDM printer. What you
actually do here is generate a geometry package and hand it to a **3D-print service** that runs
powder-bed fusion.

The outcome of this page: a vendor-ready zip of **STEP files** with the right quantities, printed
in **black PA12 nylon by SLS or MJF**. Then you move on to [assembly](/en/hardware/assembly/).

:::note[This page is about *making* the parts, not bolting them on]
You're ordering and producing the printed pieces here. Where each one goes — and how — is the
[assembly](/en/hardware/assembly/) page. This step runs **in parallel** with cutting the extrusion
and ordering off-the-shelf parts, not after them, because the print queue is one of the longest
lead times in the whole build.
:::

## What you need: 9 designs, 11 pieces

There are **9 unique designs** but **11 physical pieces**, because the idler mount is printed in
multiples. Everything else is a singleton. Here's the full list and where each part lands in the
build, so you can see why it exists:

| Design | Qty | What it does in the build |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **IdlerMountFront** | 2 | Positions the front GT2 idlers on the gantry (part of the 12-idler CoreXY routing). |
| **IdlerMountMotor** | 2 | Positions the idlers at the motor/drive-pulley end. |
| **XY joint (left)** | 1 | Carries the moving gantry and stylus carriage on the MGN9H carriages — left side. |
| **XY joint (right)** | 1 | Same, right side. The two joints are mirror parts, not interchangeable. |
| **Belt clamp** | 1 | Clamps and tensions the GT2 belt at the carriage. |
| **Solenoid mount** | 1 | Holds the 12V push-pull solenoid that presses Z (the "tap"). |
| **Phone bed** | 1 | Holds the phone in the holder under the camera. |
| **Control-board holder** | 1 | Mounts the MKS DLC32 V2.1 control board to the frame. |
| **Tube holder** | 1 | Routes the PTFE/FEP tube along the gantry. |

That's `2 + 2 + 1×7 = 11` pieces. The only part you order more than one of is the idler mount, and
it comes in **two variants** — front and motor — so order **2 of each variant**, not 4 of one.

## Material and process — non-negotiable

These parts must be made one specific way. Don't let a shop quote you something cheaper.

| Spec | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Material | **Black PA12 nylon** |
| Process | **Powder-bed fusion — SLS or MJF only** |
| Density | Solid (100%) |
| Layer height | 100 µm |
| Minimum wall | 1.0 mm |
| Finish | Raw / as-printed |

:::caution[FDM is rejected — and here's exactly why]
Several of these parts have **side-facing (cross) holes** and **overhangs**. On an FDM printer the
side holes deform as the plastic is laid down, and the overhangs sag without support — so the MGN9H
carriages won't seat, the solenoid won't sit square, and the belt clamp won't grip. SLS and MJF
build the part inside a **powder bed** with no supports, so overhangs and holes come out true in
every direction. PA12 nylon via SLS or MJF, full stop. A home FDM machine is not an option for
these parts.
:::

## Where the geometry comes from

The authoritative geometry is the **build123d Python part definitions** in the repo. You run an
exporter that turns them into **STEP files**, and the STEP files are what you send the shop.

:::caution[Do not send the legacy FreeCAD files]
`hardware/custom/` contains five old `.FCStd` files — `PhysiClaw_BeltClamp`, `PhysiClaw_Pulley_Front`,
`PhysiClaw_Pulley_Motor`, `PhysiClaw_SolenoidMount`, and `PhysiClaw_XY_Joint`. These are **legacy and
not authoritative**. Do not export them, and do not hand them to a printer. The build123d → STEP
package below is the single source of truth.
:::

## Generate the STEP package

The exporter lives at `hardware/parts/export_custom.py`. It writes **one `.step` file per part** to
`hardware/output/step/`. Run it from the repo root:

```bash
uv run python -m hardware.parts.export_custom
```

This exports all 9 designs. The quantities are baked into the exporter (`IdlerMountFront` qty=2,
`IdlerMountMotor` qty=2, the other seven qty=1), so the file list mirrors the table above.

Then package the result for the vendor:

<Steps>

1. **Check the output.** Confirm `hardware/output/step/` contains a `.step` file for every design.

2. **Zip the STEP files** into a single archive to upload.

3. **Write the quantities in the order, not just the filenames.** The shop prints what you tell it,
   so spell out: IdlerMountFront ×2, IdlerMountMotor ×2, everything else ×1.

4. **Attach the material/process spec** from the table above — black PA12, SLS or MJF, solid, 100 µm,
   1.0 mm min wall, raw finish.

5. **Upload the zip to your print service** and place the order.

</Steps>

## Cost and lead time, honestly

The 3D prints run about **¥190** in China — one line of the ~$145 / ~¥1046 total parts budget. Expect
**higher in USD/EU markets**, since powder-bed printing is pricier there.

This is a **message-the-vendor, multi-day** step, not an instant one. You upload the STEP zip, confirm
the per-part quantities with the shop, then wait for the print queue and lead time. Because that wait
is long, place this order **early and in parallel** with cutting your extrusion and buying the
off-the-shelf parts — don't treat it as something you do after everything else has arrived.

:::tip[Order the prints first]
Of the three sourcing buckets, the prints and the cut extrusion have the longest lead times. Kick off
this STEP order before you start chasing fasteners and consumables, so the slow stuff is in the queue
while you handle the rest.
:::
