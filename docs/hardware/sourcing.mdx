This page is the complete parts-and-ordering reference: *what* every part is, *what it costs*, and
how to get it onto your desk — what you buy off the shelf versus what a vendor has to cut or print
for you, and the order to place things in so the slow stuff arrives in parallel. Read it before you
buy, cut, or print anything.

One thing to be straight about first: there is **no ready-made arm**. PhysiClaw's X/Y motion is
a hand-built **CoreXY gantry** you source as raw parts — extrusion, rails, belt, steppers, a
control board — and assemble yourself. Nothing arrives pre-built as "the arm," and the Z axis is
a **solenoid** you wire in, not a plotter pen. That means real sourcing work: messaging a vendor
to cut aluminium, uploading files to a 3D-print service, and yes, a little soldering for wire
splices. Budget a few days for parts to arrive, not an afternoon.

## What it costs

Plan on roughly **$145 USD** in parts (about **¥1046** in China at ~7.2 CNY/USD). The headline
is lowest if you source from the China-centric supply chain these parts come from; the same build
runs **higher in USD/EU markets**, mostly on the rails, steppers, and machined extrusion. Treat
these figures as a China-centric floor, not a fixed price.

Use these per-item references to sanity-check any quote you get (China pricing, ¥):

| Item | Approx. |
| --- | --- |
| Camera (4K/8MP autofocus USB) | ¥200 |
| 3D prints (9 designs / 11 pieces) | ¥190 |
| MGN9H rails + carriages | ¥120 |
| MKS DLC32 V2.1 control board | ¥100 |
| Aluminium extrusion (cut/tapped) | ¥60 |
| 2x NEMA 17 steppers | ¥50 |
| 12V 2A PSU | ¥50 |
| 2x TMC2209 drivers | ¥35 |

Fasteners, belt, idlers, solenoid, and consumables make up the rest. None of it is special-order
or expensive on its own — the cost is spread across a long list (screws, T-nuts, shims, and spacers
sell in 20/50/100-piece packs, so even cheap pieces add up), which is exactly why the ordering
*workflow* matters more than hunting for a deal on any single line.

## Three sourcing buckets

The full BOM is 250+ pieces, which sounds intimidating until you sort it into three actions. Every
item falls into exactly one of these:

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="A — Buy as-is" icon="rocket">
    Off-the-shelf, no modification. The bulk of the list: MGN9H rails + carriages, GT2 belt /
    pulleys / 12 idlers, 2x NEMA 17 steppers, the solenoid, the MKS DLC32 V2.1 board, 2x TMC2209
    drivers, the 12V 2A barrel PSU, the 4K/8MP autofocus USB webcam, all fasteners, and consumables.
  </Card>
  <Card title="B — Buy + get it cut" icon="setting">
    The aluminium extrusion. You don't buy stock lengths and machine them by hand — you send a
    vendor the exact cut, tap, and counterbore spec and they do it for you.
  </Card>
  <Card title="C — Send out to print" icon="document">
    The 9 custom designs (11 pieces). These go to a 3D-print service as STEP files. They must be
    printed in PA12 nylon by powder-bed fusion — FDM will not work.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

Buckets **B** and **C** have the longest lead times, so they get ordered first. The itemized
tables below are grouped by bucket; the do-this-in-this-order workflow is at the end.

## Bucket B — the cut extrusion

You need seven cut pieces from raw aluminium profile. These are **custom cut, tapped, and
drilled** — message the supplier the spec and ask them to do the machining. China-centric
extrusion vendors do this routinely and it costs little extra. All extrusion is black-anodized
and EU-profile.

| Part | Spec | Qty | Machining |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Side rails | 2040, 345 mm | 2 | Counterbored M6 |
| End rails | 2040, 190 mm | 2 | Tapped M6 |
| Gantry X-beam | 1020, 185 mm | 1 | — |
| Phone-bed supports | 1020, 230 mm | 2 | — |

Here's the same spec condensed for pasting into a vendor message:

- **2x 2040 black-anodized EU profile, 345mm** — side rails, **counterbored for M6**
- **2x 2040 black-anodized EU profile, 190mm** — end rails, **tapped M6**
- **1x 1020 profile, 185mm** — gantry X-beam
- **2x 1020 profile, 230mm** — phone-bed supports

:::caution[Get the callouts right the first time]
The counterbore-vs-tap distinction and the exact lengths are load-bearing — the frame won't
square up if they're off, and a re-cut means another multi-day wait. Re-check these against the
manual before you send the message.
:::

## Bucket C — the 3D prints

This is the part most software people have never done, so here's how to think about it: you're
handing a print service a set of geometry files and a material spec, and they ship you finished
parts. These are **load-bearing fit parts** — they carry the moving gantry, position the idlers,
and hold the solenoid square to the phone — so they have to come out dimensionally true. That rules
out a desktop FDM printer. "Produce yourself" means generate the geometry package and hand it to a
**3D-print service** that runs powder-bed fusion. Drafting that order correctly is the whole job.

### What you need: 9 designs, 11 pieces

There are **9 unique designs** but **11 physical pieces**, because the idler mount is printed in
multiples — it comes in **two variants** (front and motor), so order **2 of each variant**, not 4
of one. Everything else is a singleton (`2 + 2 + 1×7 = 11`). Here's the full list and where each
part lands in the build:

| Design | Qty | What it does in the build |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **IdlerMountFront** | 2 | Positions the front GT2 idlers on the gantry (part of the 12-idler CoreXY routing). |
| **IdlerMountMotor** | 2 | Positions the idlers at the motor/drive-pulley end. |
| **XY joint (left)** | 1 | Carries the moving gantry and stylus carriage on the MGN9H carriages — left side. |
| **XY joint (right)** | 1 | Same, right side. The two joints are mirror parts, not interchangeable. |
| **Belt clamp** | 1 | Clamps and tensions the GT2 belt at the carriage. |
| **Solenoid mount** | 1 | Holds the 12V push-pull solenoid that presses Z (the "tap"). |
| **Phone bed** | 1 | Holds the phone in the holder under the camera. |
| **Control-board holder** | 1 | Mounts the MKS DLC32 V2.1 control board to the frame. |
| **Tube holder** | 1 | Routes the PTFE/FEP tube along the gantry. |

### Material and process — non-negotiable

Specify this exactly, and don't let a shop talk you into FDM because it's cheaper:

| Spec | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Material | **Black PA12 nylon** |
| Process | **Powder-bed fusion — SLS or MJF only** |
| Density | Solid (100%) |
| Layer height | 100 µm |
| Minimum wall | 1.0 mm |
| Finish | Raw / as-printed |

:::caution[FDM is rejected — and here's exactly why]
Several of these parts have **side-facing (cross) holes** and **overhangs**. On an FDM printer the
side holes deform as the plastic is laid down, and the overhangs sag without support — so the MGN9H
carriages won't seat, the solenoid won't sit square, and the belt clamp won't grip. SLS and MJF
build the part inside a **powder bed** with no supports, so overhangs and holes come out true in
every direction. PA12 nylon via SLS or MJF, full stop. A home FDM machine is not an option for
these parts.
:::

### Generate and package the STEP files

The authoritative geometry is the **build123d Python part definitions** in the repo. The exporter
lives at `hardware/parts/export_custom.py`; it writes **one `.step` file per part** to
`hardware/output/step/`. Run it from the repo root:

```bash
uv run python -m hardware.parts.export_custom
```

This exports all 9 designs. The quantities are baked into the exporter (`IdlerMountFront` qty=2,
`IdlerMountMotor` qty=2, the other seven qty=1), so the file list mirrors the table above. Then
package the result for the vendor:

<Steps>

1. **Check the output.** Confirm `hardware/output/step/` contains a `.step` file for every design.

2. **Zip the STEP files** into a single archive to upload.

3. **Write the quantities in the order, not just the filenames.** The shop prints what you tell it,
   so spell out: IdlerMountFront ×2, IdlerMountMotor ×2, everything else ×1.

4. **Attach the material/process spec** from the table above — black PA12, SLS or MJF, solid, 100 µm,
   1.0 mm min wall, raw finish.

5. **Upload the zip to your print service** and place the order.

</Steps>

:::caution[Do not send the legacy FreeCAD files]
`hardware/custom/` contains five old `.FCStd` files — `PhysiClaw_BeltClamp`, `PhysiClaw_Pulley_Front`,
`PhysiClaw_Pulley_Motor`, `PhysiClaw_SolenoidMount`, and `PhysiClaw_XY_Joint`. These are **legacy and
not authoritative**. Do not export them, and do not hand them to a printer. The build123d → STEP
package above is the single source of truth.
:::

## Bucket A — the off-the-shelf parts

Once B and C are placed, order everything in bucket A to arrive in parallel. These are commodity
parts you buy as-is. The itemized tables below are the full list; a few notes on what's easy to
under-buy follow.

### Linear motion

| Part | Spec | Qty |
| --- | --- | --- |
| MGN9H rail (Y) | 240 mm | 2 |
| MGN9H rail (X) | 150 mm | 1 |
| MGN9H H-type carriage | 4x M3, 16×15 mm mounting pitch | 3 |

:::note[Rails ship oiled]
MGN9H rails arrive coated in shipping oil. Wipe it off and re-grease with lithium grease before
use — this is part of the [assembly](/en/hardware/assembly/) phase.
:::

### Belt and pulleys

| Part | Spec | Qty |
| --- | --- | --- |
| GT2 belt, 6 mm | 1x 5 m roll, cut into equal A and B lengths | 1 roll |
| GT2 20T drive pulley | 5 mm bore | 2 |
| GT2 20T smooth idler | — | 10 |
| GT2 20T toothed idler | — | 2 |

That is **12 idlers total** (10 smooth + 2 toothed).

### Actuation

| Part | Spec | Qty |
| --- | --- | --- |
| NEMA 17 stepper | 42-step, for X/Y | 2 |
| Solenoid (Z/touch) | 12 V 0.8 A push-pull, 10 mm stroke, 0.2–6 N, 2x M3 mounts at 15 mm pitch | 1 |
| Capacitive stylus tip | M3 female thread, ~Ø7 mm conductive head | 1 |

The stylus tip threads onto the solenoid **core**, replacing the nut the solenoid ships with. This
is the Z mechanism — there is no servo and no pen.

### Electronics

| Part | Spec | Qty |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Control board | MKS DLC32 V2.1 (+ USB cable) | 1 |
| Stepper driver | TMC2209 | 2 |
| Power supply | 12 V 2 A, barrel jack | 1 |
| USB webcam | 4K / 8 MP, autofocus | 1 |

### Fasteners and hardware (~250+ pieces)

A from-scratch gantry needs a lot of small metal. Expect **over 250 individual pieces**, sold in
20/50/100-piece packs:

- **Screws:** BHCS / FHCS / SHCS in M3, M5, M6, plus 1/4-20.
- **Nuts:** drop-in / slide-in T-nuts and square nuts.
- **Hardware:** 10x shoulder screws, 12x shims, 8x spacers.
- **Brackets:** 6 flat brackets, 2 motor brackets, 1 corner bracket.
- **Feet:** 4 bumper feet.

**Buy by the pack, over-order.** Screws are sold in 20/50/100 packs — buy by the pack and keep
spares rather than counting exact units. Running one M3 short shouldn't stall a multi-day build.

:::tip[Loose set screws are the #1 reported failure]
Buy **Loctite 243** and apply it to the drive-pulley set screws. It's a cheap insurance against
the single most common problem people hit after the build is done. Thread-lock is not optional here.
:::

### Consumables

These ship separately from the structural parts, so it's easy to forget them and then be blocked —
order them in the same batch:

| Item | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Lithium grease | NLGI 2, for the rails — they ship oiled; wipe and re-grease |
| Loctite 243 | Thread-lock — see caution above |
| Heat-shrink | For wire splices |
| PTFE/FEP tube | ~400 mm; **wire runs outside the tube**, not through it |
| Spiral wrap | Cable management |
| Lead wire | 0.3 mm² |

### Tools you need on hand

If you don't already own these, add them to the order:

- 2 / 3 / 5 mm hex keys
- An electric driver (recommended — there are a *lot* of screws)
- A **soldering iron + heat gun** — you splice wires; this is not a solder-free build
- A flat reference surface to square the frame against
- A phone tuning app, for setting belt tension later

## What actually matters

Not every part needs to be good. Spend your attention — and your budget — where it counts:

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="Precision-critical" icon="warning">
    The **linear motion** (rails and carriages), **belt and pulley** quality, and a **crisp
    solenoid** drive on Z. These determine whether a tap lands where the agent aimed and whether a
    long-press reads differently from a tap. Buy good parts, grease and align carefully, and tune
    properly.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Forgiving" icon="approve">
    The **camera** (any 4K autofocus USB webcam is fine) and the **commodity fasteners**. These you
    can source on price. But do not extend this to the whole build — this is a precision machine,
    not a plug-together commodity kit.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

## The recommended order sequence

Lead time is the long pole, so parallelize it:

<Steps>

1. **Re-read the manual and re-check the full parts list above.** Cut lengths and counterbore/tap
   callouts must be right the first time — a wrong cut or a missed tap means another multi-day wait.

2. **Place the 3D-print order (bucket C).** Export STEP → zip → upload to a print service with the
   PA12 / SLS-or-MJF spec. This is usually the longest lead time.

3. **Place the extrusion cut order (bucket B).** Message the vendor the cut/tap/counterbore spec.

4. **Order the off-the-shelf parts (bucket A).** Steppers, board, drivers, rails, belt, idlers,
   solenoid, camera, PSU, fasteners, consumables, and any tools you're missing — all in one batch
   so they arrive while the prints and extrusion are being made.

</Steps>

This is a **message-the-vendor, multi-day** process, not an instant one. The prints and the cut
extrusion have the longest lead times, so kick those off before you chase fasteners and consumables.
