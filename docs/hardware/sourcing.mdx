You have the [bill of materials](/en/hardware/bill-of-materials/) — you know *what* the parts
are. This page is the bridge to having them on your desk: what it really costs, what you buy
off the shelf versus what a vendor has to cut or print for you, and the order to place things in
so the slow stuff arrives in parallel.

One thing to be straight about first: there is **no ready-made arm**. PhysiClaw's X/Y motion is
a hand-built **CoreXY gantry** you source as raw parts — extrusion, rails, belt, steppers, a
control board — and assemble yourself. Nothing arrives pre-built as "the arm," and the Z axis is
a **solenoid** you wire in, not a plotter pen. That means real sourcing work: messaging a vendor
to cut aluminium, uploading files to a 3D-print service, and yes, a little soldering for wire
splices. Budget a few days for parts to arrive, not an afternoon.

## What it costs

Plan on roughly **$145 USD** in parts (about **¥1046** in China at ~7.2 CNY/USD). The headline
is lowest if you source from the China-centric supply chain these parts come from; the same build
runs **higher in USD/EU markets**, mostly on the rails, steppers, and machined extrusion.

Use these per-item references to sanity-check any quote you get (China pricing, ¥):

| Item | Approx. |
| --- | --- |
| Camera (4K/8MP autofocus USB) | ¥200 |
| 3D prints (9 designs / 11 pieces) | ¥190 |
| MGN9H rails + carriages | ¥120 |
| MKS DLC32 V2.1 control board | ¥100 |
| Aluminium extrusion (cut/tapped) | ¥60 |
| 2x NEMA 17 steppers | ¥50 |
| 12V 2A PSU | ¥50 |
| 2x TMC2209 drivers | ¥35 |

Fasteners, belt, idlers, solenoid, and consumables make up the rest. None of it is special-order
or expensive on its own — the cost is spread across a long list, which is exactly why the
ordering *workflow* matters more than hunting for a deal on any single line.

## Three sourcing buckets

The full BOM is 250+ pieces, which sounds intimidating until you sort it into three actions. Every
item falls into exactly one of these:

Buckets **B** and **C** have the longest lead times, so they get ordered first. The rest is the
do-this-in-this-order workflow below.

## Bucket B — order the cut extrusion

You need seven cut pieces from raw aluminium profile. These are **custom cut, tapped, and
drilled** — message the supplier the spec and ask them to do the machining. China-centric
extrusion vendors do this routinely and it costs little extra.

Here's the spec to paste into a vendor message:

- **2x 2040 black-anodized EU profile, 345mm** — side rails, **counterbored for M6**
- **2x 2040 black-anodized EU profile, 190mm** — end rails, **tapped M6**
- **1x 1020 profile, 185mm** — gantry X-beam
- **2x 1020 profile, 230mm** — phone-bed supports

:::caution[Get the callouts right the first time]
The counterbore-vs-tap distinction and the exact lengths are load-bearing — the frame won't
square up if they're off, and a re-cut means another multi-day wait. Re-check these against the
[bill of materials](/en/hardware/bill-of-materials/) before you send the message.
:::

## Bucket C — order the 3D prints

This is the part most software people have never done, so here's how to think about it: you're
handing a print service a set of geometry files and a material spec, and they ship you finished
parts. Drafting that order correctly is the whole job.

### What to send

Export the parts as **STEP files** from the build123d sources and deliver them as a single
downloadable zip to the print shop. There are **9 unique designs / 11 pieces total**:

- **Idler mount ×4** (2 front + 2 motor variants)
- XY joint (left)
- XY joint (right)
- Belt clamp
- Solenoid mount
- Phone bed
- Control-board holder
- Tube holder

:::caution[Ignore the legacy FCStd files]
`hardware/custom/` contains five `.FCStd` files. They are **not** the authoritative geometry —
do not send these to the shop. The build123d parts exported to STEP are the source of truth.
:::

### Material and process — non-negotiable

Specify this exactly, and don't let a shop talk you into FDM because it's cheaper:

| Spec | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Material | **Black PA12 nylon** |
| Process | **Powder-bed fusion (SLS or MJF) only** |
| Density | Solid |
| Layer height | 100 µm |
| Minimum wall | 1.0 mm |
| Finish | Raw |

:::caution[FDM is rejected]
These parts have side-facing holes and overhangs. On FDM printers the **side holes deform** and
the **overhangs sag**, and the carriages and solenoid won't seat. SLS/MJF builds the part in a
powder bed with no supports, so the geometry comes out true. PA12 nylon via SLS or MJF, full stop.
:::

## Bucket A — order the off-the-shelf parts

Once B and C are placed, order everything in bucket A to arrive in parallel. A few things that are
easy to under-buy:

**Fasteners — buy by the pack, over-order.** The build uses 250+ pieces: BHCS / FHCS / SHCS in
M3 / M5 / M6 plus 1/4-20, drop-in and slide-in T-nuts, square nuts, 10x shoulder screws, 12x
shims, 8x spacers, 6 flat brackets, 2 motor brackets, 1 corner bracket, and 4 bumper feet. Screws
are sold in 20 / 50 / 100 packs — buy by the pack and keep spares rather than counting exact
units. Running one M3 short shouldn't stall a multi-day build.

:::tip[Loose set screws are the #1 reported failure]
Buy **Loctite 243** and apply it to the drive-pulley set screws. It's a cheap insurance against
the single most common problem people hit after the build is done.
:::

**Consumables — order them in the same batch.** These ship separately from the structural parts,
so it's easy to forget them and then be blocked:

- Lithium grease (**NLGI 2**) — the rails ship oiled; you wipe and re-grease
- Loctite 243
- Heat-shrink tubing
- PTFE/FEP tube, **~400mm** — note the wire runs **outside** it, not through it
- Spiral wrap
- 0.3mm² lead wire

**Tools you need on hand.** If you don't already own these, add them to the order:

- 2 / 3 / 5mm hex keys
- An electric driver (recommended — there are a *lot* of screws)
- A **soldering iron + heat gun** — you splice wires; this is not a solder-free build
- A flat surface to square the frame against
- A phone tuning app, for setting belt tension later

## The recommended order sequence

Lead time is the long pole, so parallelize it:

<Steps>

1. **Re-read the manual and re-check the full BOM.** Cut lengths and counterbore/tap callouts must
   be right the first time — a wrong cut or a missed tap means another multi-day wait.

2. **Place the 3D-print order (bucket C).** Export STEP → zip → upload to a print service with the
   PA12 / SLS-or-MJF spec. This is usually the longest lead time.

3. **Place the extrusion cut order (bucket B).** Message the vendor the cut/tap/counterbore spec.

4. **Order the off-the-shelf parts (bucket A).** Steppers, board, drivers, rails, belt, idlers,
   solenoid, camera, PSU, fasteners, consumables, and any tools you're missing — all in one batch
   so they arrive while the prints and extrusion are being made.

</Steps>
