You already know the brain: an AI agent driving a real phone through the look → think → move →
confirm loop, with no APIs. This page is about the **body** — the machine that gives that agent a
finger. Before you spend any money, here is the honest picture: what the rig actually is, what it
costs, what it demands of you, and the order of work from parts to a running agent.

The short version: this is a **from-scratch, hand-built machine**, not a kit and not a ready-made
plotter. You will source parts, send custom-cut metal and 3D-print orders to vendors, assemble a
CoreXY gantry, do a little soldering, flash firmware, and calibrate. Plan for a **multi-day**
project. It's very doable — but go in clear-eyed.

## What the finished rig is

PhysiClaw is a small desktop gantry built around **2040 and 1020 aluminium extrusion**. Two
**NEMA 17 steppers** drive a **CoreXY** belt arrangement that moves a **capacitive stylus** in X
and Y across a phone lying face-up in a corner holder. The down-press — the Z axis, the actual
"touch" — is a **12V push-pull solenoid**: a coil that shoves a rod down to press the stylus, then
a spring lifts it. An **overhead 4K/8MP autofocus USB webcam** looks straight down at the screen.
An **MKS DLC32 V2.1** controller runs the motors and solenoid; it connects over **USB** to your
Mac running the `physiclaw` server, with **12V** from a barrel power supply.

## What it really costs

Roughly **$145 in parts** — about **¥1046 in China** (at ~7.2 CNY/USD), and **higher in the US/EU**
where the supply chain is less convenient. Treat this as a parts figure *before shipping and lead
times*, not a precise all-in number.

The notable line items (China pricing, for reference):

## What it really demands of you

This is a **multi-day** project, in four phases:

<Steps>

1. **Source, print, and cut.** The supply chain is China-centric. You'll message vendors to get
   extrusions custom **cut, tapped, and drilled**, and upload the STEP files to a 3D-print service.
   Expect **multi-day lead times** before you have everything in hand.

2. **Assemble.** The manual's own estimate is **about two to three hours** of mechanical assembly
   once all parts have arrived.

3. **Flash firmware.** Flash **FluidNC** to the control board through a browser. This is a hard
   prerequisite before hardware setup.

4. **Calibrate and tune.** A guided wizard teaches the rig where the screen is; tuning belt
   tension and the solenoid is iterative and a little finicky.

</Steps>

:::caution[Read first, buy second]
Read the whole manual and check the **full** [Bill of materials](/en/hardware/bill-of-materials/)
before ordering anything. Some parts need custom fabrication and have lead times, and the BOM has
fastener counts and pack sizes you'll want to batch into one order.
:::

### Real fabrication is involved

Don't expect everything to arrive ready to bolt together:

- **Cut metal.** 4× 2040 extrusions + 1× 1020 X-beam + 2× 1020 bed supports, each cut to length,
  some tapped or counterbored for M6. See [Sourcing](/en/hardware/sourcing/).
- **3D prints.** **9 unique designs / 11 pieces**, ordered from an **SLS or MJF** service in
  **black PA12 nylon**. FDM is rejected — the side holes deform and overhangs sag. See
  [Printed parts](/en/hardware/printed-parts/).
- **Fasteners.** ~250+ screws, T-nuts, shoulder screws, shims, and brackets.
- **Yes, light soldering.** You'll need a **soldering iron and a heat gun** for wire splices and
  heat-shrink. This is not a no-solder build.

matching a target frequency, like tuning a string.

## Two build steps that are easy to miss

These aren't optional polish — get them wrong and the rig won't work. Both are documented in
detail later; just know they exist before you start.

:::caution[The Z-conversion]
The solenoid ships with a nut on its core. You **remove that nut and thread the capacitive stylus
tip** (M3 female thread, ~Ø7mm conductive head) directly onto the solenoid core in its place. The
stylus *is* the Z mechanism — covered in [Assembly](/en/hardware/assembly/).
:::

:::caution[Earth-ground the stylus]
The capacitive tip must be tied to a **verified earth** — for example, a grounded PC chassis.
**Never** a wall socket, and **never** the control board's GND. Without a real earth path, the
screen won't register the taps at all. This is both a safety and a reliability requirement.
:::

## The road from parts to a running agent

The rest of the docs are this build, in order. Hardware first (each stage depends on the one
before it), then software.

<Steps>

1. **[Bill of materials](/en/hardware/bill-of-materials/)** → **[Sourcing](/en/hardware/sourcing/)**
   → **[Printed parts](/en/hardware/printed-parts/)** — order parts, get metal cut, send prints.

2. **[Assemble](/en/hardware/assembly/)** in dependency order: frame → idlers → motors → linear
   rails → belts → stylus/solenoid (mount + Z-conversion) → phone bed → control board → camera →
   wiring.

3. **[Flash firmware](/en/hardware/firmware/)** — a browser flow puts FluidNC on the MKS DLC32.
   Do this **before** hardware setup.

4. **[Tune](/en/hardware/tuning/)** — belt tension and the CoreXY motion; the iterative part.

5. **[Install the software](/en/set-up/install/)** — one command installs the `physiclaw` CLI.

6. **[Prepare the phone](/en/set-up/prepare-the-phone/)** — turn on AssistiveTouch and add three
   iOS Shortcuts.

7. **[Calibrate](/en/set-up/calibrate/)** — a guided **10-step wizard** (18 points for the arm,
   15 dots for the camera) teaches the rig where the screen is, so taps land where the agent aims.

8. **[Run your first task](/en/run/first-task/)** — point your model at PhysiClaw and give it a
   goal in plain language.

</Steps>
