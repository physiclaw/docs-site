---
title: Calibrate
description: The 10-step wizard that teaches the arm, the camera, and the screen to agree — what each step does, what "good" looks like, and the one step that saves the result.
---

Calibration is what turns three separate, hand-built devices — the CoreXY arm, the overhead
camera, and the phone screen — into one system that agrees on coordinates. None of them start
out knowing where the others are. The wizard fixes that: it measures how the arm's motion, the
camera's view, and the screen's pixels relate, so when the agent aims a tap at a button's
bounding box, the stylus actually lands on that button.

Under the hood, every gesture converts a bounding-box center (a `0–1` fraction of screen width
and height) into a real arm position in GRBL millimetres. Calibration is what fits that map —
a calibrated affine transform called `pct_to_grbl`, plus a camera homography. You don't need the
math to run the wizard; if you want it, see
[the calibration math](/en/concepts/calibration-math/).

You don't need a model for this. Calibration is pure geometry. (To actually *drive* the phone
with the agent afterward you'll add one — `physiclaw models key <provider>` then
`physiclaw models use <provider/model>` — covered in [your first task](/en/run/first-task/).)

## Before you start

- The hardware is assembled, the rig is tuned, and the stylus is earth-grounded.
- **FluidNC is flashed and verified.** The firmware flash is a prerequisite gate — the wizard
  won't connect to the arm without it.
- The [server is running](/en/set-up/install/) on port `8048`.
- The phone is [prepared](/en/set-up/prepare-the-phone/): AssistiveTouch on, three Shortcuts
  created, bridge page open and showing **PhysiClaw**.

## How to run it

Two equivalent paths drive the *same* `/api/*` steps on the running server — pick either.

<Tabs>

<TabItem label="Browser wizard">

When the server started it opened a wizard in your browser:

```text
http://localhost:8048/setup-hardware
```

Click through the steps there. If the tab didn't open, navigate to that URL.

</TabItem>

<TabItem label="CLI">

From a **second terminal** (leave the server running in the first):

```bash
physiclaw setup hardware
```

Useful flags:

- `-a` / `--auto` — **Auto mode.** Arms itself once the phone connects, then drives every step
  on its own, pausing only on errors or steps that need you to physically do something (seat the
  stylus, fix glare).
- `--trace` — after calibrating, also traces the screen border via **8 edge points** (optional;
  handy for verifying the mapped screen rectangle).

</TabItem>

</Tabs>

:::note[It only runs forward — and won't save bad numbers]
Each step *proves* something before the next one runs. If a step can't get a clean result it
**stops and tells you what to fix** (reduce glare, straighten the phone, reopen the bridge page)
instead of saving bad numbers. This forward-only gating is a quality feature, not a bug.
:::

## The 10 steps

<Steps>

1. **Connect the phone.**
   The wizard confirms the phone is paired to the bridge, showing a QR to scan if it isn't yet.
   It won't proceed until the bridge is live. **Good:** the bridge page says *PhysiClaw* and the
   wizard reports the phone connected.

2. **Position the rig.**
   Plug the control board into USB and its **12 V** power, connect the USB camera overhead
   (pointing straight down at the phone), and seat the phone in the holder with its **top-left
   corner registered** against the holder's corner — screen level and facing straight up. Keep
   the phone unlocked with the bridge page in the foreground. **Good:** nothing wobbles, the
   screen is flat, and the bridge page is visible.

3. **Connect the arm.**
   PhysiClaw scans the computer's serial ports and connects to the FluidNC/GRBL board,
   autodetecting it at **115200 baud** and sending `$I` to confirm a matching `grbl/VER`
   response (`POST /api/connect-arm`). **Good:** "Arm connected." If it can't, the usual cause
   is the USB cable or the **12 V supply being off**.

4. **Connect the camera.**
   PhysiClaw auto-detects which camera is the right one by spotting the **red/green/blue/yellow
   corner clusters** the bridge page draws — so the page must be open and the screen awake. It
   probes USB indices `0..7` and saves the winner as `cam_index`. **Good:** "Camera N
   connected." If auto-detect misses, it shows previews and asks you to pick.

5. **Locate the screen (viewport shift).**
   The phone shows a full-screen **orange square**; you **single-tap** AssistiveTouch
   (screenshot) then **double-tap** (upload). From that screenshot PhysiClaw derives the screen's
   device-pixel-ratio and offset, lining up where it *draws* with the real pixels. This **must
   come before** the touch steps. **Good:** "Screen located" — the screenshot pipeline worked
   *and* the viewport was measured.

6. **Calibrate the arm.**
   The phone shows an **orange circle at screen center**; position the stylus tip over it, then
   the arm **taps 18 points** — a 3-point probe triangle plus a 15-point grid, about **30
   seconds** — to fit `pct_to_grbl`, its tilt, and a re-origin. **Good:** "Arm calibrated —
   mapped 18 points." A *"phone looks rotated"* warning means straighten the phone before
   trusting the result.

7. **Calibrate the camera.**
   First a **rotation and diagnostic check** (keep the whole screen in view, evenly lit, no
   glare), then the camera maps **15 dots** drawn across the screen, fitting `pct_to_cam` and a
   **RANSAC homography**. It needs to find **all 15 dots** or the step fails. **Good:** rotation
   reads as expected and all 15 dots are found. Glare or a partial view is the common failure
   here.

8. **Validate.**
   The real proof. PhysiClaw picks **5 random dots**: for each, it finds the dot with the camera,
   taps where the arm thinks it is, and measures the error. A tap passes under a threshold of
   **`0.015`** (about **5 px** on a 390-px-wide screen), and the step **passes at ≥ 2 of 5**.
   **This is the only step that saves the bundle to disk** and returns the phone to bridge mode.
   **Good:** the taps land on target and "Calibration saved."

9. **Verify AssistiveTouch.**
   A final check of the **screenshot + clipboard pipeline**: PhysiClaw runs single-tap,
   double-tap, and long-press through the three Shortcuts to confirm capture and clipboard
   sync work end to end. **Good:** "Screenshot + clipboard verified."

10. **Finish.**
    PhysiClaw sends the phone home (`POST /api/phone/home`) then marks itself ready
    (`/api/ready`). **Good:** "PhysiClaw is ready" — the arm, camera, and screen are calibrated
    together, and every MCP tool is now available to your agent.

</Steps>

:::tip[Mind the terminology]
The arm step maps **18 points**; the camera and validate steps use **15 dots** and **5 dots**.
The counts are exact and they describe different things — the points the *arm* taps versus the
dots the *camera* reads.
:::

## The two steps to actually watch

**Step 6** and **step 8** are load-bearing.

- A full **18-point** arm map (step 6) means the arm sees a clean, unobstructed screen.
- **Validate** (step 8) is the real proof the camera's view and the arm's motion genuinely agree
  — and the number that gets persisted.

:::caution[Don't push through a marginal Validate]
Validate is the *only* step that writes the calibration, and that bundle is what every later tap
relies on. If the pass is marginal, **fix the lighting, re-square the rig, or re-seat the phone,
then redo the camera or arm step** — do not ignore a weak result and carry on.
:::

## Where the calibration lands

A successful Validate writes the bundle to:

```text
~/.physiclaw/calibration/bundle.json
```

(The data root also keeps a `calibration/cache/` alongside it.)

:::note[Restarting mid-wizard]
A fresh `physiclaw server` does **not** auto-restore a half-finished calibration session — it
only resumes one with `--warm-start`. If you restart the server partway through, pass
`--warm-start` or expect to start the wizard over.
:::

## Re-calibrate when…

Calibration captures the exact geometry of *this* rig in *this* spot. Run `physiclaw setup
hardware` again whenever that geometry changes:

:::caution[Anything physical moved]
- **You moved or re-aimed the camera** — even a small nudge shifts every mapping.
- **You swapped the phone** (or its case), changing the screen size or seating.
- **You bumped the rig** or re-seated the phone off its corner registration.
- **Taps start landing slightly off** — a sign drift has crept in. Re-validate.
:::

If a step keeps failing, the cause is almost always physical — glare, a rotated phone, a loose
USB cable, the 12 V supply off, or the bridge page asleep. The
[troubleshooting guide](/en/troubleshooting/) and the [CLI reference](/en/reference/cli/) cover
the rest.
