---
title: Calibration math
description: How a 0–1 bbox becomes a stylus that lands on the right pixel — the screen-fraction → millimetre chain, the two maps learned during setup, and why taps drift.
---

A tap lands where it should because PhysiClaw learns, **once**, how three coordinate systems line
up: the brain's **screen fractions**, the camera's **pixels**, and the arm's **millimetres**.
Calibration is the half-minute where the rig measures those relationships and bakes them into a
couple of small matrices; every gesture afterward is just multiplying a bbox center through them.
This page is the intuition and a little of the math — for the reader who watched
[setup](/en/set-up/calibrate/) run and wants to *understand* it, not re-run it.

## Three coordinate systems

Everything a tap touches lives in one of three spaces, and the whole job of calibration is
translating between them:

```text
  screen 0–1            camera pixels          arm millimetres
 (what the brain        (what the lens         (what the gantry
  reasons in)            actually sees)         actually moves in)

 [0.55, 0.59]  ─────────── A (pct_to_grbl) ────────►  (gx, gy) mm
       ▲
       └──── B (pct_to_cam) ◄────  pixel  ────  (camera frame)
        listing comes back in 0–1, from camera pixels
```

- **Screen 0–1** — the portable fractions the [bbox listing](/en/concepts/how-it-sees/) uses.
  `(0,0)` is the top-left of the phone screen, `(1,1)` the bottom-right. This is the *only* space
  the brain ever speaks.
- **Camera pixels** — where a thing appears in the overhead frame. Depends entirely on where you
  mounted the camera, so it's meaningless until measured.
- **Arm millimetres** — GRBL coordinates the gantry actually drives to. Also meaningless until
  measured, because it depends on where the phone sits under the arm.

PhysiClaw learns two maps to bridge them. **Map A** (screen → arm, called `pct_to_grbl`) so it can
*act*; **Map B** (screen → camera, `pct_to_cam`) so it can *see* — turn a detected camera pixel
back into a `0–1` screen fraction for the listing. Keep the hook: **A is for acting, B is for
seeing.**

## The chain a single tap follows

When the brain says `tap [0.51, 0.55, 0.59, 0.63]`, here's the actual arithmetic:

<Steps>

1. **Bbox → center.** Average the corners: `cx = (0.51 + 0.59) / 2 = 0.55`,
   `cy = (0.55 + 0.63) / 2 = 0.59`. You're aiming at the middle of the box.

2. **Screen → millimetres (Map A).** The orchestrator multiplies that `(0.55, 0.59)` through the
   `pct_to_grbl` affine to get GRBL coordinates `(gx, gy)` in mm.

3. **Move and strike.** The gantry fast-moves to `(gx, gy)`; the solenoid fires its
   [hit-and-hold](/en/reference/gestures/); the tip lands.

</Steps>

That second step is the bridge between the [12 MCP tools](/en/reference/mcp-tools/) and the
physical rig. `tap`, `double_tap`, `long_press`, and `swipe` all funnel through the same
conversion: the orchestrator takes the bbox center in `0–1` percent, runs it through `pct_to_grbl`,
and only then calls `arm.tap` / `arm.double_tap` / `arm.long_press` / `arm.swipe_to`. The brain
never sees a millimetre.

The `peek` path runs the same idea in reverse: the overhead camera sees the screen in *pixels*, and
**Map B** turns those pixels back into `0–1` screen fractions so the listing the brain reads is in
the same space as the bbox it'll tap. A is for acting, B is for seeing.

## What an affine map actually is

Map A is an **affine transform** — the simplest mapping that can stretch, rotate, skew, and shift
a plane while keeping straight lines straight. It's stored as a 2×3 matrix and applied like this:

```text
┌ gx ┐   ┌ a  b  c ┐   ┌ screen_x ┐
│    │ = │         │ · │ screen_y │
└ gy ┘   └ d  e  f ┘   └    1     │
```

That's six numbers. The `a b / d e` block handles scale, rotation, and skew — how much arm-X
travel one unit of screen-X costs, and how tilted the phone is relative to the gantry. The `c f`
column is the offset — where screen `(0,0)` sits in arm space. Six numbers are enough because a
phone lying flat under an overhead camera is, to a very good approximation, a flat plane viewed
flat: no perspective warp and no lens distortion to model, just a tilt-and-stretch. That's why a
handful of measured points can pin the whole screen.

## Learning Map A — 18 points the arm taps on itself

The arm can't see the screen, so it finds the screen → arm map by **tapping and listening**: the
phone reports back exactly where each touch landed (in screen `0–1`) and the arm knows where it
was in millimetres, giving one exact (millimetre, screen-fraction) pair per tap. Enough pairs fix
the affine.

<Steps>

1. **Probe triangle (3 taps).** Tap at arm origin `(0,0)`, then `+10 mm` along X, then `+10 mm`
   along Y. Three points are the minimum to bootstrap a *rough* affine — just enough to predict
   roughly where the rest of the screen is.

2. **Grid (15 taps).** Using that rough map, the arm visits a 3-column × 5-row grid spread across
   the screen and taps each cell. Every tap adds another exact (millimetre, screen-fraction) pair.

3. **Fit + re-origin.** All **18 points** are least-squares fit into the final, accurate Map A,
   along with the tilt. The arm then re-declares screen-center as its `(0,0)`, so the whole
   coordinate frame is anchored to the phone, not to wherever the gantry happened to home.

</Steps>

The whole sequence takes **about 30 seconds** — it's the grid you watched the arm tap on itself.

:::caution[The tip must be earth-grounded for any of this to work]
The phone only registers a capacitive touch if the stylus tip is tied to a verified earth. If
[grounding](/en/hardware/assembly/) is missing, taps don't register and no (mm, screen-fraction)
pairs come back — calibration can't even start. Because the solenoid is fixed-stroke, there is no
touch *depth* to find; a missed tap just means flaky contact, and the rig re-fires.
:::

The fit also yields a free diagnostic: a **tilt ratio** measuring how skewed the phone is relative
to arm travel. Near `0` means the phone is square to the gantry; a high value warns you to
straighten it before continuing.

## Learning Map B — 15 dots the camera reads

Map B (screen → camera) is learned the opposite way: the phone *displays* a known pattern and the
camera *looks*. The bridge page lights up a **15-dot grid** — bright red dots at known screen
fractions — and the overhead camera detects each one's pixel position, giving 15
(screen-fraction, camera-pixel) pairs. The step needs **all 15 dots** detected before it'll fit.

The fit is a little sturdier than Map A. PhysiClaw fits both the `pct_to_cam` mapping and a
**RANSAC homography** — a robust fit that ignores the occasional mis-detected dot rather than
letting it skew the whole map. The flat-plane intuition still holds (a phone screen seen from
overhead is close to flat), but the homography is what makes the camera mapping survive a stray
glint or shadow. The result is what `peek` needs: any camera pixel can be turned back into a screen
fraction for the listing.

:::note[Why the screenshot path needs its own pre-step]
Before either touch step, the wizard's **viewport shift** (step 5, and it *must* come first) flashes
one orange square at a known spot and reads the phone's own screenshot back. From that it derives
the device pixel ratio (`dpr`) and the status-bar / safe-area offset. That reconciles "where the
web bridge page thinks a point is" with "where it lands in the phone's native screenshot," keeping
taps accurate on the `screenshot` path too. Get this wrong and Maps A and B would be fit against a
mis-located reference — which is why it gates the touch steps.
:::

## Parking off-screen

Between every action the arm retreats to a fixed spot just **off** the phone — screen-fraction
roughly `(-0.1, -0.05)`, a little left of and above the top-left corner. Off-screen coordinates are
perfectly legal here: the same Map A that turns `(0.55, 0.59)` into millimetres turns `(-0.1,
-0.05)` into millimetres too — the affine doesn't care that the point is past the screen edge, the
matrix multiply works the same. The park spot matters for two reasons: it keeps the stylus out of
the camera's view so the next `peek` is unobstructed, and it's a known, repeatable resting position
the rig can re-anchor to after a restart.

## Validation, drift, and when to recalibrate

The last measured step **validates** the result: it taps 5 random dots and checks each landed
within `0.015` of screen width of its target — about **5 pixels on a 390-px-wide screen** — and
passes at **≥ 2 of 5**. This is also the *only* wizard step that **saves the calibration bundle to
disk**, so an unvalidated run leaves nothing behind.

Both maps assume the camera, the arm, and the phone all stayed exactly where they were when the
points were measured. Anything that moves a piece of the rig invalidates that, and taps start
landing off:

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="The rig got bumped" icon="warning">
    Knocked the arm or shifted the phone in its holder, and Map A is now wrong — the millimetres no
    longer correspond to the same screen fractions. Taps land consistently *offset*.
  </Card>
  <Card title="The camera moved" icon="warning">
    Nudged or refocused the overhead camera, and Map B is now wrong — pixels map to the wrong
    fractions, so the brain aims at a slightly wrong target even before the arm moves.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

The tell is a **systematic** miss: taps land a consistent distance off in the same direction. That
points at a *stale* transform — the fix is to [recalibrate](/en/set-up/calibrate/) and re-measure
the points so the matrices match the rig's new reality. **Random** misses in different directions
are a different problem: flaky contact or grounding, not a stale map. Mount everything rigidly and
you'll rarely touch calibration again.

:::tip[Where the result lives]
The fitted maps are saved as a calibration bundle at `~/.physiclaw/calibration/bundle.json` (the
data root keeps `calibration/{bundle.json,cache/}`). That single file is what "baked in once"
actually means — and the server only reuses a saved bundle when you start it with `--warm-start`;
otherwise it expects a fresh calibration.
:::
